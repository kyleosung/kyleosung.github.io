Content

Week Description Blank Notes T02 Notes T02 Video T03 Notes T03 Video Enrichment!
1 No tutorials yet! Please fill out the Entry Form and prepare for tutorials to start in Week 2. Thank you!
2 We review some topics from precalculus: trig identities, functions without elementary inverses, and solving exponential and logarithmic equations.
3 We learn all about approaches: limits, continuity, and the Intermediate Value Theorem. We also define the derivative and use it to find tangent lines.
4 Differentiation requires rules (Power, Product, Quotient, and Chain). Let's learn them and apply them to Newton's Method and Implicit Functions.
5 We continue to look at derivatives and their applications, focusing on inverse functions, hyperbolic functions, logarithmic differentiation, and the Extreme Value Theorem.
6 Let's investigate tricky problems that we might not have come across yet, including higher order derivatives and domains of nested logarithm functions. We also review our right angle trigonometry and prepare for our first test.
7 We return to applications of the derivative: optimization, L'Hopital's Rule, and the Mean Value Theorem.
8 What happens when we add up a large number of terms? Gauss's Trick and telescoping sums help us approximate area using a collection of rectangles called Riemann Sums. Can we find a pattern to help us evaluate these areas more quickly?
9 After learning about the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we learn about how antidifferentiation can make our lives easier (and the challenges that come with it!).
10 Let's develop more techniques to evaluate integrals. We discover that in addition to our techniques to reverse the power and chain rules, there is a way to reverse the product rule.
11 With our newfound knowledge of integrals, we consider applications like finding volumes and the average value of a function. We see that there is a new way to express the Mean Value Theorem.
12 Trigonometry helps us simplify integrals with a new type of substitution. We also integrate rational functions using partial fractions, and integrate over arcs to wrap up the course.
